The Challenge: Rising Seas, Pressing Threats

This section provides an overview of Maryland's significant vulnerability to coastal hazards, primarily driven by accelerating sea-level rise. Explore key statistics and projections that illustrate the scale of the challenge the state is confronting.

Maryland's Unique Vulnerability

Maryland's extensive coastline, over 7,000 miles along the Atlantic and Chesapeake Bay, makes it exceptionally susceptible to coastal storms, high tide flooding, and erosion. The Chesapeake Bay region is one of the most vulnerable areas in the nation to sea-level rise, a threat compounded by local land subsidence.

Impacted Lives & Lands: The Tangible Effects

Sea-level rise and increased coastal hazards are not distant threats; they are already inflicting severe and tangible impacts on Maryland's communities and ecosystems. This section explores these effects, from nuisance flooding to economic disruption and the particular vulnerabilities of specific areas.

Frequent Nuisance Flooding

Coastal communities are experiencing more frequent and severe nuisance tidal flooding, inundating roads, disrupting access to services, and impacting local economies. Baltimore, for example, saw a significant number of high-tide flooding days, with projections showing a dramatic increase.

Shoreline Erosion and Marsh Loss

Rising waters accelerate shoreline erosion, and vital natural buffers like marshes are disappearing. Thirteen Bay islands have vanished in the last century.

  • Up to 90% of coastal marshes could be lost by 2100.
  • Blackwater National Wildlife Refuge (Dorchester Co.) has lost up to 5,000 acres of marsh.

Saltwater Intrusion

A growing threat, especially on the Eastern Shore, saltwater intrusion damages agricultural fields, leading to crop loss and "ghost forests," impacting food production and local economies.

Threats to Infrastructure & Economy

Even smaller storms cause expensive property damage. Nearly all of Maryland's 2.2 million coastal properties are vulnerable. Over 100 communities may face chronic inundation by the end of the century. Smaller communities often lack resources to adapt, creating disparities.

Forging Resilience: Initiatives and Projects

In response to these challenges, Maryland has adopted a comprehensive approach involving state-led programs, collaborative partnerships, and innovative tools. This section highlights key initiatives aimed at building a more resilient coastline.

Key State-Led Programs & Policies

Coastal Resilience Program (CoastSmart)

Managed by DNR, assists communities with coastal hazards (high tide flooding, storm surge, SLR) through information, tools, training, and financial aid.

Maryland Sea Level Rise Projections

DNR & MD Sea Grant develop guidance using latest SLR projections (e.g., 2023 update) for planning and regulatory decisions.

Nuisance Flood Plans

Mandated for counties/municipalities with nuisance tidal flooding; updated every 5 years (next by Oct 1, 2025).

Coastal Resilience Easements

Novel conservation easements on private lands incorporating climate change, facilitating marsh migration, requiring hazard management plans, and establishing development setbacks.

Wetland Adaptation Area (WAA) Mapping

Uses SLAMM models to identify and prioritize areas for wetland migration, guiding land protection efforts.

Established Land Conservation Programs

Includes MET Conservation Easements, Agricultural Land Preservation, Program Open Space, and Rural Legacy programs, often leveraged for resilience.

Major Collaborative & Partner Initiatives (TNC Highlighted)

TNC in Crisfield: Model for Resilience

NOAA-funded project (since 2022) assessing flood adaptation, building local capacity, developing early warning systems, mapping drainage, and exploring nature-based solutions.

TNC: Community Outreach (COEF)

Forums identifying barriers (inaccessible funding, capacity gaps) and proposing solutions (leveraging USACE funds, private funding).

TNC: Ecological Effects of SLR (EESLR)

Project quantifying tidal wetlands' role in reducing storm surge (e.g., first few feet of marsh reduce wave height up to 90%). Data informs conservation.

TNC: REPI Challenge

$3M DoD grant (2020) with NAS Patuxent River & USFWS to purchase easements on 4,000 acres (Eastern Shore) for marsh migration and preventing incompatible development.

TNC: Resilient Protection Frameworks

$2M Lockheed Martin partnership to update easements/management plans for SLR, ensuring marsh migration, protecting 4,000 acres by 2025.

Key Collaborative Tools & Resources

  • Climate Mapping for Resilience & Adaptation (U.S. Coastal Resilience Toolkit): Real-time maps of hazards, exposure, federal policies, and funding.

Investing in Tomorrow: The Financial Landscape

Addressing Maryland's coastal challenges requires substantial financial investment. This section examines the economic impacts of coastal hazards, current funding mechanisms, and identified gaps that need to be addressed for a resilient future.

The Staggering Cost of Inaction

$27.4 Billion

Conservative estimate to safeguard MD communities from chronic flooding alone by 2040 (moderate SLR).

Historical costs are also immense:

  • $10B - $20B: Estimated recovery costs from 85 extreme weather events (1980-2024).
  • $156M+: Federal public assistance since 2011 for 6 disaster declarations (excludes nuisance flood costs).

Conversely, nature-based solutions offer economic benefits: Maryland's wetlands saved over $40 million in avoided damages from Hurricane Sandy.

Funding Gaps and Challenges

A significant disparity exists between projected needs and current investment levels. Key challenges include:

  • Lack of accessible funding for local communities.
  • Capacity gaps in local governments.
  • Complex state and federal grant processes.
  • Potential vulnerability of long-term funding to broader budget pressures.

Restoring the Bay: Focus on Water Quality

The health of the Chesapeake Bay is inextricably linked to its water quality. This section delves into the major pollutants, their sources, and Maryland's comprehensive initiatives to clean up and protect this vital estuary.

Nutrients (N & P)

Sources: Agricultural runoff (animal waste, crops), urban/suburban runoff (lawns, impervious surfaces), wastewater treatment plants.

Impacts: Fuel algae blooms, deplete dissolved oxygen, create "dead zones" harmful to aquatic life.

Sediment

Sources: Agricultural/urban runoff, erosion.

Impacts: Blocks sunlight for underwater grasses (SAV), destroying habitat and reducing the Bay's natural filtering capacity.

Chemical Contaminants

Sources: Wastewater, industries, atmospheric deposition, runoff.

Impacts: Accumulate in sediments and animal tissues, posing risks to wildlife and potentially human health.

Maryland's Cleanup Efforts & TMDL Targets

Maryland's Chesapeake Bay Cleanup Plan includes the Total Maximum Daily Load (TMDL) – a "pollution diet" for the Bay. Significant reductions are needed:

Key Initiatives:

  • Watershed Implementation Plans (WIPs): Detailed state plans to meet TMDL goals, refined in phases.
  • Two-Year Milestones: Short-term commitments to ensure continuous progress.
  • Best Management Practices (BMPs): Actions, policies, structures (e.g., cover crops, stormwater ponds) to reduce pollution locally.
  • Chesapeake Bay Legacy Act: Establishes Maryland Leaders in Environmentally Engaged Farming (LEEF) Program to incentivize agricultural conservation and improve water quality monitoring.
  • Funding Mechanisms: Water Quality Revolving Loan Fund (WQRLF), Bay Restoration Fund (BRF) support improvement projects.

Focus on Vulnerability: Maryland's Hotspots

Certain coastal areas in Maryland are disproportionately affected by sea-level rise and flooding due to their low elevation, proximity to the Bay, and socio-economic factors. This section highlights some of the most vulnerable communities and regions, drawing from Appendix A of the report.

Threats: Frequent nuisance flooding, economic disruption, property damage.

Notes: Low elevation, model for resilience strategies (early warning, drainage mapping, nature-based solutions).

Baltimore City

Threats: High-tide flooding, urban inundation.

Notes: Projected 50-155 flooding days by 2050 (up from 11 in 2019). Critical infrastructure at risk.

Dorchester County

Threats: Marsh loss, saltwater intrusion, flooding.

Notes: Home to Blackwater NWR (5,000 acres marsh loss). High rates of SLR & subsidence.

Somerset County

Threats: Marsh loss, saltwater intrusion, flooding.

Notes: Agricultural impacts. High rates of SLR & subsidence. Includes Crisfield.

Worcester County

Threats: Flooding, SLR, impacts on tourism economy.

Notes: Identified as highly at-risk. Coastal environment is bedrock of tourism.

General Eastern Shore

Threats: Widespread saltwater intrusion in agricultural areas, "ghost forests."

Notes: Focus on land protection and marsh migration corridors.

Pathways to Resilience: Key Recommendations

To accelerate coastal resilience, the report proposes several strategic recommendations. These aim to build on existing strengths, address challenges, and foster a more adaptive future for Maryland's coastal regions.

  • Establish a dedicated, long-term Climate Resilience Fund (e.g., via climate bonds, transfer taxes).
  • Simplify and centralize grant application processes through a user-friendly portal.
  • Increase technical assistance and capacity building for local governments in grant writing and project design.
  • Expand investment in wetland restoration and protect marsh migration corridors (accelerate Coastal Resilience Easements, WAA protection).
  • Integrate green infrastructure (permeable pavements, rain gardens) into urban/suburban planning.
  • Mandate and fund regular updates to vulnerability assessments using latest tools, considering socio-economic factors.
  • Enhance real-time flood monitoring and early warning systems across vulnerable communities.
  • Invest in long-term monitoring of resilience project effectiveness, especially NBS.
  • Formalize and expand collaborative platforms (like COEF) for knowledge sharing and coordination.
  • Integrate climate resilience into all state planning, capital improvement, and permitting processes.
  • Empower local communities, especially the most impacted, in decision-making for resilience projects.
